#Slide: 2
#Header: What is Diwali?
#Content: Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is a five-day Hindu festival celebrating the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. It typically falls in October or November.

#Slide: 3
#Header: Significance of Diwali
#Content: Diwali symbolizes the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya after defeating Ravana. It also honors Goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth and prosperity, inviting blessings into homes.

#Slide: 4
#Header: Traditions and Rituals
#Content: Key traditions include lighting diyas (lamps), decorating homes, performing prayers (puja), and bursting fireworks. Each day has its unique significance and rituals.

#Slide: 5
#Header: Diwali Celebrations
#Content: Diwali is celebrated with enthusiasm through family gatherings, cultural performances, and community events. Homes are adorned with lights and rangoli (art patterns).

#Slide: 6
#Header: Foods and Sweets
#Content: Sweets like ladoos, barfis, and gulab jamun are central to Diwali. Families prepare special meals and share treats with neighbors and friends to spread joy.

#Slide: 7
#Header: Diwali Around the World
#Content: Diwali is celebrated globally, especially in India, Nepal, and among Indian communities worldwide. Festivals feature cultural programs and unite diverse communities.

#Slide: 8
#Header: Environmental Impact
#Content: Diwali's fireworks contribute to air pollution. Eco-friendly celebrations are encouraged, focusing on sustainable practices, such as using less harmful fireworks and eco-friendly decorations.

#Slide: 9
#Header: summary
#Content: Diwali is a vibrant festival celebrating light, unity, and prosperity. With rich traditions and global recognition, it's a time for joy, reflection, and a commitment to sustainability.
